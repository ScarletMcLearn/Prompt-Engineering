Alternative Approaches Pattern

Brainstorming is one of the most powerful and practical ways to use a large language model. You have a problem to solve, and within seconds you can generate multiple possible solutions, compare them, and refine your thinking.

That is exactly what the Alternative Approaches Pattern is designed for.

Its core idea is simple:
If a task can be solved in more than one way, the model should propose multiple alternatives instead of a single answer.

Not every idea will be strong. Some may be impractical. But just like in traditional brainstorming, the value lies in generating options. Comparing alternatives helps clarify the problem and often leads to a better final decision.

Large language models have been trained on vast amounts of text. They have “seen” many problems and many solution strategies. Even if they have never encountered your exact situation, they can synthesize alternative approaches based on related patterns.

Basic Pattern Formulation

You can introduce the pattern with a general instruction such as:

From now on, whenever there are alternative ways to achieve the same goal, list the best alternative approaches. Compare and contrast them, and ask me which one I would like to use.

In real-world applications, this instruction should be tailored to your specific task and include more context. But even in this simple form, the pattern already changes how the model responds.

Example 1: Writing a Prompt to Detect a Leap Year

Previously, we worked on creating a prompt that determines whether a year (in YYYY-MM-DD format) is a leap year.

Now we ask the model:

Write several prompts for yourself that use few-shot examples to determine whether a year is a leap year. The output must be either “The year is a leap year” or “The year is not a leap year.”

The model may generate different approaches.

Approach 1: Structured Format

A formal few-shot structure:

Date: 2020-01-01  
Answer: The year is a leap year  

Date: 2023-01-01  
Answer: The year is not a leap year

Approach 2: Conversational Format

A dialogue-style structure:

User: Is 2020-01-01 a leap year?  
Assistant: The year is a leap year.  

User: Is 2023-01-01 a leap year?  
Assistant: The year is not a leap year.

The model can then explain the differences:

  • The first approach is more structured and suitable for automated systems.

  • The second is more conversational and works better in interactive scenarios.

Important lesson:
The model may introduce small inconsistencies or formatting errors. Generated prompts must always be reviewed and validated. The pattern expands options — it does not replace responsibility.

Example 2: Analyzing an Email Thread

We modify the pattern:

Whenever I ask you to write a prompt for a task, first list alternative ways to solve it. Then write a separate prompt for each approach.

Task:

Write a prompt that automatically detects questions in an email thread and summarizes participants’ positions under each question.

The model might suggest three approaches:

Approach 1: Analytical Extraction

  • Identify all questions.

  • Extract related responses.

  • Summarize positions in bullet points.

Approach 2: Persona Pattern

  • Act as a business communication analyst.

  • Analyze the thread and summarize key questions and viewpoints.

Approach 3: Case Report Format

  • Treat the thread as a case study.

  • Produce a structured report including:

    • List of questions

    • Positions of participants

The “case study” framing is often unexpected — yet effective. This illustrates how the pattern helps expand thinking beyond obvious solutions.

Evaluating Alternatives

Once alternatives are generated, the next question is:
Which one is best?

You can apply the model again in a separate context to evaluate the generated prompts, for example:

Write a prompt that evaluates multiple prompts designed to summarize questions and positions in an email thread.

The model may propose:

  • Efficiency-based evaluation

  • Persona-based expert evaluation

  • Improvement-focused critique

Each approach emphasizes different criteria.

Why This Pattern Matters

The Alternative Approaches Pattern:

  • Encourages structured brainstorming

  • Expands solution space

  • Reveals hidden assumptions

  • Supports comparison and trade-off analysis

  • Improves strategic thinking

It does not mean outsourcing thinking to the model.
You remain responsible for the final decision.

But as a cognitive multiplier, this pattern is extremely powerful.

Format of the Alternative Approaches Pattern

To implement this pattern, include the following core instructions in your prompt:

  • If there are alternative ways to accomplish task X, list the best alternative approaches.

  • Compare and contrast the pros and cons of each approach.

  • Include my original approach in the comparison.

  • Ask me which approach I would like to use.

Replace “X” with your specific task.
