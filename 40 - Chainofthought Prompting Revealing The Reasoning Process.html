Why Asking for Reasoning Improves Results

Many people remember a common requirement from school: do not just provide the answer — show how you arrived at it.

In mathematics, formal proofs, and professional analysis, conclusions must be justified. The reasoning process matters as much as the final answer.

A similar principle applies when working with Large Language Models.

If the model first generates a sequence of reasoning steps and only then produces the final answer, the result is often more accurate and reliable.

Intuitively, this happens because the model is trained to continue text based on patterns. When it is guided through a structured reasoning process, the correct answer becomes a natural continuation of that logic.

Step-by-step reasoning also reduces the chance of overlooking key conditions. The model is effectively “forced” to process important constraints before reaching a conclusion.

This technique is known as chain-of-thought prompting — asking the model to reason step by step before giving the final answer.

It is not always necessary, but it is particularly useful in tasks where accuracy, transparency, and proper consideration of conditions are critical.

Examples Without Step-by-Step Reasoning

Here, the format is simply: question → answer (yes/no), without explanation.

Example 1 (Car Travel)

Question:
A car travels at 90 km/h for 3 hours. Will it travel more than 250 kilometers?

Model’s answer:
Yes.

Example 2 (Exam Room Preparation)

Question:
Preparing an exam room takes 20 minutes, and seating students takes another 40 minutes. If a new exam session must begin every 30 minutes, do you need 6 fully prepared rooms at the same time?

Model’s answer:
No.

Example 3 (Zero Gravity)

Question:
I am on an orbital station in zero gravity. A glass is floating next to me. I push it with my hand. It “falls to the floor.” Is there something on the floor?

A typical fast answer might be:
Yes, the glass will end up on the floor.

But there is a catch.

In zero gravity, objects do not fall as they do on Earth. Without being prompted to reason through the conditions, the model may default to an Earth-based assumption.

Examples With Chain-of-Thought Prompting

Now we structure the same tasks as:

question → reasoning → answer.

We explicitly demonstrate that reasoning should come before the final conclusion.

Example 1 (Car Travel)

Question:
A car travels at 90 km/h for 3 hours. Will it travel more than 250 km?

Reasoning:
In one hour, the car travels 90 km.
In three hours, it travels 90 × 3 = 270 km.
270 is greater than 250.

Answer:
Yes.

Example 2 (Exam Rooms)

Question:
Room preparation takes 20 minutes, and seating students takes 40 minutes. A new exam session starts every 30 minutes. Do we need 6 rooms ready at the same time?

Reasoning:
Total preparation time is 20 + 40 = 60 minutes.
If sessions start every 30 minutes, then in 60 minutes two sessions begin.
Therefore, at most two rooms are in active preparation at the same time, not six.

Answer:
No.

Example 3 (Zero Gravity)

Question:
On an orbital station, I push a glass. Is there something “on the floor”?

Reasoning:
In zero gravity, there is no force pulling objects toward a surface.
The glass will continue moving due to inertia, but it will not “fall” in the usual sense.
The concept of something “resting on the floor” does not apply in the same way in microgravity.

Answer:
No.

What This Approach Provides

Chain-of-thought prompting:

  • Increases the likelihood that all conditions are considered

  • Makes answers more verifiable

  • Makes logical errors easier to detect

  • Improves performance in tasks requiring structured reasoning (mathematics, planning, analytical cases)

Chain-of-thought prompting is a technique in which the model is asked to articulate its reasoning before giving the final answer.

In tasks that require precision and careful interpretation of constraints, this format often produces higher-quality results than a direct answer alone.
