Audience Persona Pattern

Previously, we discussed the Persona Pattern — where we tell the model to “act as” someone, and it begins to reason and respond from that role. That technique defines who is speaking.

The Audience Persona Pattern is different.

Here, we define who the explanation is for.

Instead of changing the role of the model, we describe the intended listener.

We simply say:

Explain this as if I were…

The model then automatically adjusts:

  • level of complexity

  • vocabulary

  • tone

  • examples

  • metaphors

  • depth of explanation

Without long formatting instructions, we shape the response by defining the audience.

Example 1: Someone Without a Technical Background

Prompt:

Explain what large language models are and how they work. Assume I have no background in computer science.

The model responds with a simple explanation.
It might describe an LLM as a system that reads a large number of texts and learns patterns in language — like a very advanced predictive text system.

No technical jargon. Minimal complexity. High clarity.

Example 2: A Historical Explorer

Prompt:

Explain what large language models are. Imagine I am a 15th-century explorer.

Instead of referencing computers directly, the model may compare an LLM to:

  • a master scribe

  • a vast library

  • a scholar who has studied thousands of manuscripts

This is not technical adaptation — it is cultural and conceptual adaptation.

We did not manually describe tone, vocabulary, or metaphor rules.
We simply changed the audience.

Example 3: A Second-Grade Student Who Gets Bored Easily

Prompt:

Explain what large language models are as if I were a second-grade student who gets bored quickly.

Now the explanation changes again:

  • shorter sentences

  • energetic tone

  • playful examples

  • simplified language

The model does not just simplify.
It adjusts emotional pacing and structure.

Example 4: Mathematics-Only Explanation

Prompt:

Explain what large language models are. I accept explanations only in mathematical form.

Now the response becomes formal and abstract:

  • probability distributions

  • parameters

  • optimization functions

  • statistical prediction

Same topic. Completely different framing.

What Is Happening?

We are not writing detailed formatting rules.

Instead, we define the audience.

The model retrieves learned communication patterns associated with that type of audience and adapts automatically.

If the Persona Pattern controls who speaks,
the Audience Persona Pattern controls who listens.

Key Takeaway

The Audience Persona Pattern allows you to:

  • automatically adjust complexity

  • change tone without strict formatting rules

  • generate alternative interpretations

  • increase relevance

  • tailor communication precisely

Sometimes one short sentence —
“Assume I am…” —
can radically change the quality of the output.

Audience Persona Pattern Structure

To use this pattern, your prompt should include:

  • Explain X to me.

  • Assume that I am Y.

Where:

  • X = topic

  • Y = audience persona

Example:

Explain what large language models are. Assume I am a healthcare executive.
