The Flipped Interaction Pattern

Sometimes we need a Large Language Model to ask us questions and guide the problem-solving process.

We do not always know which steps are required to achieve a goal. In such cases, it is logical to transfer initiative to the model and allow it to lead the interaction through clarifying questions.

This approach is called the Flipped Interaction Pattern.

What Is the Core Idea?

Normally, we ask questions and the model answers.

Here, we reverse the roles:

The model asks questions — we respond.

We “flip” the interaction.

This pattern can be used:

  • To achieve a specific goal (e.g., building a training plan)

  • For diagnosing a problem

  • For creating a personalized solution

  • For testing or quizzes

  • For games

  • In any situation where we do not want to formulate the right questions ourselves

It is especially useful when we do not know all the details required to solve the task.

Basic Structure

Example prompt:

Ask me questions about my fitness goals until you have enough information to design a strength training program. When you have enough information, provide the program. Ask the first question.

This structure includes three essential components:

  1. Instruct the model to ask questions.

  2. Define the final goal.

  3. Tell it to start with the first question.

Clearly defining the goal is crucial. The better the model understands the outcome, the better its questions will be.

One Question at a Time or a List?

You can instruct the model to:

  • Ask questions one at a time

  • Ask a fixed number of questions

  • Provide a list of questions

  • Continue asking until enough information is gathered

If you want step-by-step interaction, a reliable technique is to end the prompt with:

Ask the first question.

This usually triggers sequential mode.

Example Walkthrough

The model might begin with:

What are your specific fitness goals? Are you aiming to build muscle, increase strength, improve endurance, or reach a specific performance target?

Suppose you respond:

I want to improve explosive power and prevent knee pain related to frequent jumping during cycling.

The model may then ask:

  • Do you have current or past injuries?

  • How many days per week can you train?

  • Do you have access to gym equipment?

  • Has a medical professional diagnosed your condition, or are you aiming at prevention?

You continue answering. Once the model determines it has enough information, it generates a structured training plan.

Why Does This Work?

The model:

  • Incorporates your responses

  • Connects them logically

  • Applies learned patterns from similar training data

  • Suggests exercises commonly associated with similar goals

For example, when addressing knee stability or impact-related strain, structured strength exercises and stabilization movements are commonly recommended.

If you do not know which questions to ask, the model can structure the information-gathering process for you.

The Key Mechanism

The critical instruction in the example was:

Ask questions until you have enough information.

This is the strength of the pattern.

The model determines when sufficient data has been gathered and only then provides the final output.

This helps avoid:

  • Overly generic answers

  • “I need more information” responses

  • Incomplete recommendations

Where Else Can This Pattern Be Applied?

  • Customer support automation (collect data → then provide solution)

  • Technical diagnostics

  • Travel planning

  • Meal planning

  • Project design

  • Learning and assessment

  • Game scenarios

Anywhere you need to:

  • Collect data

  • Analyze it

  • Make a decision

Why Is the Model Often Good at Asking Questions?

Because it has been trained on vast amounts of text and has seen common interaction patterns:

  • What questions are asked during medical consultations

  • What details are clarified when designing training plans

  • What data is collected during troubleshooting

  • What criteria are evaluated in decision-making

You may not know those patterns — the model does.

That is why the Flipped Interaction Pattern is powerful when you struggle to structure the clarification process yourself.

If you need the model to understand the situation first — let it ask the questions.

Formal Pattern Structure

To apply this pattern, your prompt should include:

  1. A goal (X):

    I want you to ask me questions to achieve X.

  2. A stopping condition (Y):

    Continue asking questions until condition Y is satisfied.

  3. Interaction format:

    Ask one question at a time.
    Ask the first question.

Replace X with a concrete objective, such as:

  • Creating a meal plan

  • Designing marketing variations

  • Diagnosing a technical issue

Define Y clearly, for example:

  • Until you have enough information about my audience and goals

  • Until you understand my dietary preferences and calorie targets

Example 1

I want you to ask me questions that will help generate variations of my marketing materials. Continue asking until you have enough information about my current messaging, audience, and goals. Ask the first question.

Example 2

I want you to ask me questions to diagnose an issue with my internet connection. Continue asking until you have enough information to identify the two most likely causes. Ask one question at a time. Ask the first question.
