Few-Shot Prompting with Intermediate Steps

Few-shot examples do not have to be limited to a simple “input → output” format.
We can include intermediate steps that lead to the final result.

Why is this interesting?

When we demonstrate intermediate steps, a Large Language Model can learn to apply a sequence of actions to reach a goal. Instead of simply mapping “input → answer” or “situation → action,” we enable more complex behavior:

If a situation occurs → what should happen next → and then next → and so on — until the desired state is reached.

If examples are structured correctly, the model can learn to break a problem into steps and determine how to approach it.

Example: Expanding the Driving Scenario

Let’s revisit our driving example and enrich it.

Now we introduce a sequence:

Situation → Thought → Action → Thought → Action → …

Example 1

Situation:
I am driving at 60 miles per hour and see the brake lights of the car ahead turn on.

Thought:
I need to reduce speed before I collide with the vehicle in front of me.

Action:
Press the brake pedal.

Thought:
The car may not stop in time.

Action:
Check whether the shoulder is wide enough to move into.

Thought:
The shoulder is wide enough.

Action:
Steer onto the shoulder to avoid collision.

Now the same initial situation includes reasoning steps and decision points.

Example 2

Situation:
I have just entered the highway from an on-ramp and am traveling at 30 miles per hour.

Thought:
I need to accelerate to match traffic speed to avoid being rear-ended.

Action:
Press the accelerator pedal.

Thought:
I have reached the appropriate speed.

Action:
Ease off the accelerator and maintain speed.

Again, the situation is broken into multiple steps.

Testing the Pattern

Now we provide a new situation and leave space for continuation:

Situation:
I am reversing out of a parking space and notice that the vehicle behind me has its reverse lights on.

Action:

Instead of a short answer like “Stop and wait,” the model continues in our structured format.

Action:
Stop immediately and pause reversing.

Thought:
I need to assess the surroundings to determine whether it is safe to continue.

Action:
Check mirrors and surrounding space for obstacles or pedestrians.

Thought:
It is safe to continue reversing.

Action:
Resume reversing slowly while continuing to monitor the environment.

The output becomes more detailed and step-based.

Where This Can Be Applied

There are many possible applications.

For example, customer support:

A customer reports a problem → we know the typical diagnostic steps → through few-shot examples, the model can learn to guide the customer through a structured troubleshooting process.

If the model sees enough variations, it can choose the appropriate trajectory depending on the customer’s response.

Generating More Examples

As before, we can ask the model to generate additional examples.

For instance:

Situation:
I am driving on a narrow winding road with multiple blind curves.

Thought:
I need to reduce speed and stay alert to avoid a collision.

Action:
Slow down and maintain a safe position within the lane.

Thought:
The road ahead becomes straight and visibility improves.

Action:
Gradually return to normal driving speed.

Another example:

Thought:
My fuel level is very low.

Thought:
I need to find a gas station before the fuel runs out.

Action:
Check the GPS or navigation system for the nearest gas station.

Thought:
The nearest station is farther than expected.

Action:
Reduce speed to conserve fuel and reassess available options.

We begin to see something close to a scenario script — a step-by-step solution unfolding.

Increasing Complexity: New Information Changes the Path

Consider this continuation:

Action:
Pull over safely, turn on hazard lights, and assess options.

Thought:
Roadside assistance is unavailable, and the nearest gas station is too far to reach on foot.

What happens next?

Thought:
I need to conserve my phone battery and find assistance as efficiently as possible.

Action:
Look for nearby houses or businesses where I could request help.

Thought:
I notice a house in the distance.

The model continues constructing the scenario.

Why This Works

Intuitively, this works because the model predicts what comes next once it recognizes a pattern.

It sees a structure like:

“Situation:”
“Thought:”
“Action:”

and continues it.

In training data, models have seen many structured narratives, scripts, and procedural explanations:

Event → internal reasoning → action → consequence.

When we reproduce that structure consistently, the model extends it naturally.

About Formatting

If we want the model to clearly recognize the pattern, it is better to:

  • Use simple, consistent prefixes (“Situation,” “Thought,” “Action”)

  • Keep the structure clean and readable

  • Avoid breaking the pattern with long unstructured blocks of text

If, for example, we write “Action:” and then include a long paragraph without consistent formatting, the pattern becomes harder for the model to follow.

The Main Idea

By showing intermediate steps, we enable the model to:

  • Continue a scenario step by step

  • Avoid requiring us to manually encode all rules

  • Select the next action based on a recognized pattern

This is the strength of the few-shot approach:
Instead of long instructions and rigid rules, we provide several high-quality examples — and the model extends the behavior from there.
