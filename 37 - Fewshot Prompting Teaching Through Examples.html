Few-shot prompting is a powerful technique you can use when designing prompts.

The core idea is simple:
you show the model several examples of input data and the corresponding expected output.

In other words:

  • You provide an input example

  • You show what the correct output should look like

  • Then you provide another input

  • And again show the correct output

By doing this, you establish a pattern that the model begins to follow.

In practice, you are “teaching” the model inside the prompt itself.
You are not describing the rules in words — you are demonstrating the desired behavior through examples.

Instead of saying “Do it this way,” you provide samples and rely on the model to continue the pattern.

A Simple Few-Shot Example

Few-shot prompts often use structural prefixes such as:

Input:
Output:
Sentiment:

The prefixes can vary. The structure can be more complex. But the essential idea is to demonstrate a template.

Suppose we want to perform sentiment analysis.

The task: determine whether a review is positive, negative, or neutral.

Instead of writing:

“Analyze the comments and tell me whether they are positive or not,”

we provide several examples.

Examples

Input:
“The movie was interesting but felt too long.”
Sentiment: Neutral

(There is both a positive and a negative element — balanced overall.)

Input:
“I completely disliked this book. It lacked important details and logical structure.”
Sentiment: Negative

Input:
“This book helped me understand the topic clearly and was written in a very accessible way.”
Sentiment: Positive

Now we provide a new input:

Input:
“I wasn’t sure at first whether I liked this restaurant. The service was slow, but the food turned out to be excellent.”
Sentiment:

The model continues the pattern:

Neutral

Why?

Because it observed:

  • A negative element (slow service)

  • A positive element (excellent food)

  • In previous examples, that balance resulted in “Neutral”

We never explicitly explained the rule.
The model inferred it from the examples.

An Important Observation

Notice something critical.

We:

  • Did not describe the classification algorithm

  • Did not provide strict written instructions such as “choose one of three categories”

  • Did not separately list the allowed output values

Yet the model:

  • Restricted its answer to the three observed categories (Positive, Negative, Neutral)

  • Preserved the “Input → Sentiment” structure

  • Continued the established pattern correctly

Why does this happen?

Because Large Language Models are trained to predict the next segment of text based on learned patterns.

When the model sees a repeated structure like:

Input:
...text...
Sentiment: ...

it begins to treat this as a template.

When a new “Input:” appears, it expects “Sentiment:” followed by one of the previously observed labels.

Another Example

Input:
“This coffee was terrible — over-roasted and bitter.”

Even if we do not explicitly write “Sentiment:” afterward, the model will often continue the pattern automatically:

Sentiment: Negative

It reproduces the discovered format and completes the sequence according to earlier examples.

Why Few-Shot Works

Few-shot prompting is especially effective when:

  • The rules are difficult to formalize in words

  • A strict output format is required

  • The range of possible answers must be limited

You already have examples of correct task solutions

In this approach, we do not explain how to perform the task.
We demonstrate the expected result. The model detects the pattern and continues it.


Planning with Few-Shot Prompting

Large Language Models are capable of a certain level of planning — and this is an interesting capability. Using few-shot examples, they can learn to recognize patterns and determine what action should follow in a given situation.

I am not showing this example because models are limited to tasks like sentiment analysis or simple classification. I want to broaden your understanding of what few-shot prompting can do. This technique can be adapted to a wide range of problems.

We already saw sentiment analysis. Now let’s look at a very different type of task — decision-making in driving situations.

Example: Situation → Action

Situation:
I am driving at 60 miles per hour and see the brake lights of the car ahead turn on.
Action: Brake.

This is the first example in our few-shot set.

Situation:
I have just entered a highway from an on-ramp and am driving at 30 miles per hour.
Action: Accelerate.

We are “teaching” the model that it will receive a situation and must determine the appropriate action.

Situation:
A deer runs onto the road in front of the car. I am driving at 15 miles per hour, and there is a wide shoulder on the right.
Action: Brake and steer toward the shoulder.

Situation:
I am reversing out of a parking space and notice that the vehicle behind me has its reverse lights on.
Action:

We leave the “Action:” line blank. The model continues the pattern.

The model responds:

Action: Stop and wait until the other vehicle completes its maneuver before proceeding.

It makes a reasonable decision based on the pattern.

Important clarification:
I am not suggesting building autonomous driving systems using few-shot prompting. That is not the point.

The point is that we should not think narrowly in terms of classification tasks only.

Few-shot prompting allows us to solve structured mapping problems such as:

  • Situation → Action

  • Context → Decision

  • Conditions → Strategy

Generating Additional Examples

We can go further and ask:

“Please provide more examples.”

Then again begin with:

Situation:

The model continues the pattern.

For example:

Situation:
You are driving in heavy rain and notice that the road ahead appears flooded.
Action: Slow down and assess whether it is safe to proceed.

Personally, I am not certain this is always the safest decision. In some cases, stopping completely may be more appropriate. This reminds us that responsibility for evaluating model output always lies with the user.

Another example:

Situation:
You are driving on a two-lane road and an oncoming vehicle has its high beams on.
Action: Avoid looking directly at the headlights and maintain your lane position.

We observe that the model:

  • Preserves structure

  • Generates new situations

  • Selects corresponding actions

  • Continues the established pattern

One Model Can Help Train Another

An interesting point:

We can generate many examples using a model, then manually review and refine them, and use them as training material.

In some research contexts, outputs from one model have been used to improve or fine-tune another model.

In this way, models can help generate few-shot examples for other systems.
